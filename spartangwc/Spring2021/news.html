Week 10

Only the Beginning

Last Saturday marked the end of the Spring 2021 edition of Spartan Girls Who Code. Yet Saturday was only the beginning of something bigger.

Saturday marked the end of one leg in our journey toward learning computer science, but was not the final destination. Learning to code is a lifelong journey—even our mentors and advisors are still years away from the destination! That's the beauty of computer science, though—no matter who you are, how old you are, where you're from, or what you do, you can embark on the journey, learning and growing with each step. The destination may be the long-term goal, but it isn't the short-term point. The challenges you overcome, the views you enjoy, and the friends you make are. Enjoying the journey is what it's all about—and we've certainly done that with you all this spring.

We'd like to thank our students for their energy and enthusiasm; our students' parents for their ongoing support and encouragement; our mentors for making each Saturday exciting, engaging, and entertaining; and our advisors for their organization, coordination and commitment to making SGWC a reality. Without you all, we wouldn't be here.

Advisor Spotlight

Spartan Girls Who Code wouldn't be possible without the hard work of our advisors, Teresa Isela VanderSloot, Sebnem Onsay, and Dr. Laura Dillon. As such, we wanted to feature them in our final newsletter! Join us in thanking them for making SGWC a reality.



Teresa Isela VanderSloot



Sebnem Onsay

What do you do at MSU?
I'm the Director of Women in Engineering, Recruitment and K-12 Outreach!

How did you become interested in computer science, and how did you get to where you are today?
I was the advisor for computer science major for over ten years. In this role, I met with hundreds of students and it was evident there were barely any women and other underrepresented students in the major. This spurred me and a colleague to help start a book club, which eventually became our MSU Women in Computing club. I also helped start a statewide conference called MICWIC and a non-profit organization, iTEC. The aim of these programs was to increase the diversity in CS. As a result of my work, the Dean created my current position and asked me to take on this role.

What's your favorite part about Spartan Girls Who Code?
I enjoy observing the mentor/mentee relationships. I also love to see the students getting excited as they are learning new concepts and gaining confidence.

What message would you like to leave with our students and mentors moving forward?
Dream big! Believe in your future, don't be afraid to ask for help along the way and utilize your networks. You will meet people who genuinely want you to succeed. Explore and learn CS. Computational literacy is a necessary skill for your future. Your ideas, thoughts and problem solving skills are needed at the table.

What do you do at MSU?
I'm a Teaching Professor, and teach Algorithms and Data Structures in the Department of Computer Science and Engineering!

How did you become interested in computer science, and how did you get to where you are today?
I came to the USA with a B.S degree in Pharmacy in 1990. We relocated and resided in East Lansing MI—MSU did not have a Graduate School program for Pharmacy, so I decided to pursue another degree. I love Mathematics and Physics so I decided to attend college for the second time, this time pursuing Bachelor of Science in Computer Science. I then continued to pursue my graduate degree in this area.

What's your favorite part about Spartan Girls Who Code?
Seeing young people showing incredible enthusiasm for computing, seeing their reactions to their running program. I absolutely love being part of Spartan Girls Who Code.

What message would you like to leave with our students and mentors moving forward?
Learning is fun. Allow yourselves to be challenged, learn new tools and techniques in computing, ask a lot of questions. It is ok to fail—you can always start again!




Laura Dillon

What do you do at MSU?
I'm a Professor Emeritus—which means I'm retired! At MSU, I mainly taught introduction to programming and software engineering. In the last 5 years before retiring, I created and taught the Computational Thinking Lab. I LOVED teaching this class and recruited many Technovation and SGWC mentors from it. Now I work with MSU mentors and Teresa and Sebnem on coding clubs. I also volunteer for many programs of the National Center for Women in Information Technology (NCWIT).

How did you become interested in computer science, and how did you get to where you are today?
Completely by accident. I was studying math at University of Michigan, when I decided to take a year off to see the USA. In that year, I had a chance to teach programming in the business school at Eastern Montana College. Learning Cobol and Basic a week before my students was a bit harrowing, but I really liked programming. I liked problem-solving and I liked that programming was useful (a common "use" given for homology theory was showing that you can't comb a hairy ball flat without creating a cowlick!). When my graduate advisor died unexpectedly while I was away, I decided to switch both majors and schools. I got my PhD in CS from UMass Amherst five years later. I taught at UMass the next year since delivering a baby within a semester of starting a new job didn't sound like a smart thing to do. The following year I took a job at University of California Santa Barbara (UCSB), where I taught and did research in software engineering, and my husband started a PhD program in the ecology of lakes. We ended up back in Michigan 12 years later when MSU solved our two-body problem by offering both my husband and I jobs. As a professor at MSU, I got to travel the world to give talks and teach programming and software engineering. A favorite was three summer camps for secondary school girls and their teachers in Rwanda!

What's your favorite part about Spartan Girls Who Code?
The coders! Especially coders who don't have a clue at the start and are amazed to learn what they can do with code by the end. It is so much fun to see the light gradually turn on! I love seeing them gain confidence as they realize that programming just takes practice.

What message would you like to leave with our students and mentors moving forward?
My favorite Grace Hopper quote: "A ship in port is safe, but that's not what ships are built for."

It's been a pleasure spending the spring together. Enjoy the journey!

-The SGWC Team

Meeting Recap

At this point, you can probably recite our weekly meeting routine—so it's about time we switched things up! This week's meeting featured our first-ever live, virtual spotlight presentation by Zosha Korzecke, a December 2020 MSU graduate, former Spartan Girls Who Code Mentor, and current Software Engineer at Google!

To wake up for the morning and give Zosha the chance to meet students in small group settings, we opened our meeting with a "Would You Rather?" icebreaker powered by Google Jamboard, a collaboration platform similar to Google Slides. Mentors shared would-you-rather questions over Gather and Zoom, and students moved a virtual sticky note with their name on it between option (a) and option (b) on the Jamboard to indicate their preference. Before moving between questions, mentors and participants shared their thoughts out loud, debating the relative merits of option (a) over option (b), and vice-versa. All in all, the icebreaker was a hit!


A Google Jamboard-powered game of "Would You Rather?"

A few of the most popular questions are listed below. They'll make for an interesting dinner table conversation!

  • Would you rather fight 100 duck-sized horses or 1 horse-sized duck?
  • Would you rather have an elephant-sized hamster or a hamster-sized elephant?
  • Would you rather have a pet alligator or a pet talking almond?
  • Do you think you would last longer against a shark or a tiger?

With a silly icebreaker and plenty of laughs behind us, we jumped right into Zosha's spotlight—and we were blown away. Zosha's wisdom, optimism and encouragement spoke to students and mentors alike, inspiring us all in a time where it's all too easy to become discouraged. Sharing her journey which combines a passion for art with a passion for computer science, she reminded us that anything is possible with code—the only limits are the ones we impose on ourselves.

Zosha also introduced the notion of imposter syndrome in computer science, in which a programmer feels she's not as smart or capable as those around her, and shared tips for overcoming such thoughts. Perhaps most importantly, Zosha reminded us that we all belong in computer science, despite whatever stereotypes, expectations, or social constructs may make us feel we're not the "typical" computer scientist. It's about time we changed the face of computer science!


A snapshot of Zosha's journey from MSU to Google.

After spotlight, students jumped back into their final projects and spent the remaining meeting time building and tweaking their creations. Some students even finished their first final project idea and began a second creation! Mentors floated between groups, checking in to make sure students were on track to finish by the end of next week's 3/20 meeting.

We can't wait for students to share their creations with parents and siblings at the Final Project Fair on 3/27! Stay tuned for more details regarding the Final Project Fair in next week's newsletter.

    4. Gather.Town

    Let's face it: we're all tired of Zoom these days. So, we'll be using a new platform for the Spring 2021 session of SGWC: Gather. We think it will make for a great online learning experience! You can find more information in Gather's documentation, but we provide a brief summary below.

    Gather is built around the idea of virtual proximity: you can move into and out of conversations in a virtual space by moving an avatar of yourself. When your avatar is near another person's avatar, their video and audio will connect with yours and you'll be able to chat with them. When your avatars move apart, your video and audio will disconnect. This makes it easy for multiple groups of people to hold separate conversations at the same time without the hassle of breakout rooms. In a sense, it recreates in-person interactions!

    We'll be giving a full tutorial and demo of Gather at our first meeting, so don't stress about it! We think you'll like it much better than Zoom.
